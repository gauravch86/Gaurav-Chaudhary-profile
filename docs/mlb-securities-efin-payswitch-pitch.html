1. RBI — Regulatory & financial mandates

Non-bank fintechs providing payment gateway services must operate as regulated intermediaries under the Payment and Settlement Systems Act, 2007.

  • Payment Aggregator (PA) license: Formal RBI authorization required to operate as a PA for merchant payment aggregation.
  • Net worth: Minimum ₹15 crore at application; scale to ₹25 crore within the regulatory timeline and maintain continuously.
  • Data localisation: All payment data (txn logs, credentials, UPI metadata) stored only in India — no overseas mirroring (DwarkaSwitch architecture: India-only processing & storage).
  • Escrow account: Scheduled commercial bank escrow to pool and route merchant funds, segregated from corporate capital.
  • Fit & proper: Promoters and directors must meet RBI governance and integrity standards.

2. NPCI — Architecture & ecosystem onboarding

Non-banks cannot connect directly to the central UPI switch; onboarding is always multi-party.

  • Sponsor bank partnership: One or more NPCI-approved PSP sponsor banks underwrite traffic and settlement.
  • TPAP registration: If the gateway uses a dedicated merchant/consumer app checkout (E-Fin’s case), TPAP licensing via sponsor bank with tri-partite bank–NPCI–provider agreement.
  • Volume caps: Consumer-facing apps must comply with NPCI market-share caps (e.g. ~30% UPI volume threshold) to limit concentration risk.

Why an NBFC / lender cannot be a direct PSP — and what E-Fin does instead

No — an NBFC cannot be a direct UPI PSP. RBI/NPCI reserve the PSP role for banking institutions that hold settlement core accounts and clearing-house liquidity. NBFCs are non-banks and cannot operate demand deposit accounts or clear net settlements on interbank rails.

How E-Fin participates today (and with PaySwitch):

  • As TPAP / app provider: E-Fin’s lending app offers UPI checkout and payouts, but all switch traffic routes through an approved sponsor PSP bank (ICICI, Axis, HDFC, SBI, etc.).
  • As PA-style merchant flows: Onboarding and business compliance sit with the gateway/PA layer; underlying UPI txn execution uses partner bank APIs — PayU today, PaySwitch tomorrow with fewer intermediaries.
  • Credit on UPI: NBFC-issued credit over UPI requires co-brand with a scheduled commercial bank or PPI-wallet linkage — relevant for future E-Fin product extensions, not core EMI collect.
Strategic takeaway: Replacing PayU is not about bypassing regulation — it is about shortening the regulated chain. PaySwitch sits adjacent to the sponsor bank’s switch connection (DwarkaSwitch), giving E-Fin better economics, faster settlement, and higher success rates while remaining fully within RBI/NPCI architecture.

Regulator-Aware Two-Bank Architecture

A cooperative bank (DCCB sponsor) is strategic for switch certification and inclusion rails — but NPCI/RBI constraints limit using a cooperative bank alone for high-volume fintech merchant settlement. PaySwitch uses a hybrid routing design (honest, audit-ready).

Why hybrid?

  • DCCB constraints: Many DCCBs are NFS sub-members — cannot sponsor third-party PG acquiring at scale; settlement liquidity limits for multi-crore payday peaks.
  • Cooperative sweet spot: Inclusion-focused flows — verification, small-value payouts — at lower unit economics (0.8X vs. aggregator pricing).
  • Commercial bank: ICICI / Axis / HDFC / Yes Bank-style nodal setup for core UPI collect + bulk disbursals NPCI clears for fintech merchants.
  • Reusable IP: Message parsing, ISO 8583, encryption, webhooks — same DwarkaSwitch engine; only routing rules differ.
